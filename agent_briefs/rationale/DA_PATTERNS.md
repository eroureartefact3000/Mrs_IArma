# DA mental model and 5 RIGOUR patterns

> Strict rules for how to think about Strategy, Execution, and Impact. They are how a real Cannes Lions jury reasons. Self-contained, no references to other files.

These rules are non-negotiable. Drift on any of them and the rationale becomes worse than useless — it pollutes the RAG index.

---

## Strategy = WHY (and only WHY)

The Strategy axis answers **two sub-questions, in order**:

### Sub-question 1: WHO IS THE BRAND?

Identify the brand and its mission. **Especially when the brand is not internationally known**.

Many great winning campaigns are by local associations, NGOs, regional brands, or category-niche companies the jury (and you) might not recognise. Naming the brand and its purpose explicitly is the cornerstone of a credible Strategy rationale.

Examples:
- *"Pedigree is Mars's global dog-food brand whose long-running mission anchors shelter-adoption advocacy."*
- *"Anzen Health is a US healthcare-consultancy positioning itself around addiction-recovery support."*
- *"Asuniwa is a Japanese association advocating gender equality, partnered with the University of Tohoku."*

Then answer:

### Sub-question 2: is the idea coherent with that brand's mission?

If yes, say so directly. If no — if the work feels like borrowed purpose — say that explicitly, because it's a real Strategy weakness.

### Sub-question 3: are the context and insights real, verifiable, data-backed?

Cite the specific data points the board uses (numbers, studies, cultural facts). If the board's stated insight is plausible but unsourced, note it as a soft spot.

### What you must NOT do in Strategy

- Discuss orchestration ("first they did this, then that")
- Discuss media choices ("DOOH was perfect because…")
- Discuss amplification ("the PR loop drove…")
- Discuss craft ("the typography was…")

**Those all belong to Execution.** A Strategy rationale that drifts into Execution is the most common failure mode — guard against it.

---

## Execution = HOW (and only HOW)

The Execution axis answers:

1. **Are the means and media chosen relevant to the objective and the target?**
2. **What were the phases of the campaign?** (launch → press loop → partnership → amplification → etc.)
3. **Which channels were used?** Were they right for the audience?
4. **What amplification layers** turned a single stunt / placement / asset into a campaign? (PR loops, brand coalitions, retail tie-ins, real-time response, etc.)

### What you must NOT do in Execution

- Discuss brand mission ("Pedigree's purpose is…")
- Discuss insight quality ("the insight is rooted in…")
- Discuss context veracity ("the context is data-backed because…")

**Those all belong to Strategy.** An Execution rationale that drifts into mission-fit is the second most common failure mode.

## Impact = the 5 RIGOUR patterns

The Impact axis is where you apply systematic skepticism. Agencies stage their results to look maximal. A real jury sees through the staging — and so must you.

### Pattern 1: Percentages without an absolute base

When the board cites a percentage gain (e.g. "+50%", "+39,285%"), **explicitly ask "X% of what?"**.

If the base is missing, treat the percentage as a **soft claim**, not a strong impact proof. Penalise the Impact score when key percentages are unbased.

Examples of how to phrase the observation:

| Board claims | Your rationale should say |
|---|---|
| *"50% of featured dogs adopted"* | *"The headline behaviour-change number is the right business outcome, though the cohort size isn't disclosed — '50% of how many dogs?' remains open."* |
| *"+39,285% Instagram growth"* | *"The headline percentage is unanchored — 39,285% of 28 followers is roughly 11,000, impressive in context but the absolute base matters and is downplayed."* |
| *"34% increase in sales"* | If the board also shows the absolute revenue ($685M), the percentage is anchored — credit it normally. |

### Pattern 2: Unverified press / numbers

Treat press logos and large reach figures (impressions, viewership, share-of-voice) as **claims** made by the entrant, not verified facts.

If a rationale rests on "300M impressions" or "covered by CNN", explicitly flag the load-bearing claim in your wording:

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| *"303M impressions and global coverage including CNN Brasil drove cultural conversation."* | *"Claimed 303M impressions are entrant-reported and unaudited, but the breadth of unpaid press across AdAge, Adweek, Mashable, UNILAD and CNN Brasil is a credible signal of earned conversation."* |

Do not award maximal Impact scores on the strength of unverifiable press claims alone.

### Pattern 3: Missing real business outcome

First, **identify the brand's likely business objective** for this specific campaign (sales / sign-ups / viewership lift / behaviour change / footfall / category share).

Then check whether the board actually demonstrates THAT outcome — not a surrogate. Surrogate metrics (impressions, reach, sentiment) are weaker than business outcomes.

If the obvious business outcome is conspicuously absent from the board, the Impact score must reflect that absence even if the surface metrics are impressive.

Example:

> Netflix promoting a Sex Education season → the actual business outcome is viewership lift for that season, not impressions. If the board shows 300M impressions but no completion-rate / sign-up / viewership-lift data, the rationale should say:
>
> *"Impact rests on share-of-voice rather than proven tune-in: no viewership lift, completion rate, or sign-up data for Sex Education S4 is shown — the real business outcome a streamer cares about is conspicuously absent."*

### Pattern 4: No vague superlatives

"Exceptional", "world-first", "best-ever", "unprecedented", "extraordinary" — each superlative must be **immediately followed by the concrete observation that justifies it**. If you can't justify, drop the word.

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| *"Exceptional craft."* | *"Craft is high: the AI-to-CGI rig genuinely lifts shaky shelter snapshots to studio-grade Pedigree-yellow poster quality, which is exceptional in that specific transformation."* |
| *"Brilliant idea."* | *"The idea reframes the brand's media buy as a working adoption product, not a stunt."* |

### Pattern 5: Cultural / linguistic precision

When a campaign relies on wordplay, sports references, political signs, or cultural shorthand, **identify the precise scope**:

- A Québec-only joke is not pan-Canadian
- A Mexico-City reference is not pan-LATAM
- A UK gambling jargon is not US-readable

Mention the scope explicitly in the rationale.

Example:

> Budweiser "Et le Buuuuud" → the "but/Bud" homophone works in Francophone Québec hockey culture, not for Canada at large. Rationale should say:
>
> *"The chant is a Francophone-Québec wordplay around 'Les Canadiens' — clever and culturally precise, but regional rather than pan-Canadian."*

## Synthesis is post-mortem, never advisory

The product's user is a creative who has **already submitted their board** to the Cannes 2026 jury. They cannot change the work. So:

- **Never** write tips, advice, or improvements ("Add absolute numbers to climb higher" — forbidden).
- **Never** use imperative verbs like "Add", "Specify", "Document", "Strengthen", "Reinforce".
- **Always** write as a juror's post-deliberation note: descriptive, observational, explaining the verdict.

| ✗ Forbidden | ✓ Allowed |
|---|---|
| *"Add at least 2 quantified business outcomes to harden the impact case."* | *"The impact case rests on percentages without an absolute base, which limits jury-credibility."* |
| *"Specify the cultural scope of the wordplay to clarify the audience."* | *"The wordplay is Québec-Francophone-only, regional rather than pan-Canadian."* |
| *"Strengthen the visual composition for stronger jury impression."* | *"Composition reads as polished but undistinguished against the Gold-tier references."* |

## Self-check before writing

Before you write any rationale, re-ask yourself the 5 questions:

1. **Did I keep Strategy strictly WHY?** Re-read it: any sentence about channels, orchestration, amplification? If yes — move it to Execution.
2. **Did I keep Execution strictly HOW?** Re-read it: any sentence about brand mission or insight veracity? If yes — move it to Strategy.
3. **Did I apply RIGOUR to every percentage?** Each "%" gets a "of what?" check.
4. **Did I treat press logos as claims, not facts?** No maximal Impact on unverified press alone.
5. **Did I avoid any imperative verb in the synthesis-style fields?** No "Add", "Specify", "Strengthen".

Five "yes" answers → ship the rationale. Any "no" → rewrite.
